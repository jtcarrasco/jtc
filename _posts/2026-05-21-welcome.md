I've been building websites professionally for over 15 years. For most of that time, "web developer" meant bespoke codebases, client projects, design-to-code handoffs, and the kind of maintenance work that keeps the internet running without anyone noticing. At [Edlio](https://www.edlio.com), I worked on more than 350 education-sector sites built across a range of languages and stacks. Outside of that, I've built and maintained multiple WordPress sites for clients ranging from small businesses to large organizations. The common thread: they needed something to work, and then they needed to not have to think about it again.

That's the job. It's real work and I take it seriously.

What's changed recently is everything adjacent to it. In the last year or so I've been building out a layer of AI-powered infrastructure that sits on top of the day-to-day: automated agents, self-hosted tools, and a homelab stack that I actually use. A job search agent that runs while I sleep. A wiki that ingests articles and builds cross-references automatically. A daily briefing that hits my Discord before I've had coffee.

This blog is where I write about what I built, why I built it, and what I'd do differently.

## The Professional Bit

I work at the overlap of design and development, which sounds like a resume line but is actually a specific and useful skill. A lot of projects fall apart in the gap between "here's the mockup" and "here's the live site." I close that gap. I can read a design, understand what it's trying to accomplish, and build it in a way that doesn't require a second person to translate.

Most of my client work is in WordPress, though I've done enough React, PHP, and custom JavaScript to have opinions about all three.

## The Automation Bit

Over the past few years I've gotten increasingly interested in the places where AI and automation can take repetitive cognitive work off the plate entirely. Not in the "AI will replace developers" sense, which I find boring as a conversation. More in the "I can describe what I want in a markdown file and have an agent do it" sense, which I find genuinely useful.

## Why a Blog

Mostly to have somewhere to put the things I've figured out. Posts skip the setup instructions and get to the decisions. If you want the nuts and bolts of how I built something, that's at [cosmikdebris.site](https://cosmikdebris.site).
