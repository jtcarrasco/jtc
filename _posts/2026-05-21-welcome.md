I've been a web developer for over a decade. For most of that time, "web developer" meant WordPress sites, client projects, design-to-code handoffs, and the kind of maintenance work that keeps the internet running without anyone noticing. I'm good at it. I've managed upward of 60 client sites at once, done full redesigns, and built out production pipelines for teams that needed someone who could sit at the intersection of design and code without things catching fire.

That's the job. It's real work and I take it seriously.

What's changed recently is everything adjacent to it. In the last year or so I've been building out a layer of AI-powered infrastructure that sits on top of the day-to-day: automated agents, self-hosted tools, and a homelab stack that I use to run all of it. Not as side projects in the abstract, but as tools I actually use. A job search agent that runs while I sleep. A wiki that ingests articles and builds cross-references automatically. A daily briefing that hits my Discord before I've had coffee.

This blog is where I write about what I built, why I built it, and what I'd do differently.

## The Professional Bit

I specialize in the overlap between design and development, which sounds like a resume line but is actually a specific and useful skill. A lot of projects fall apart in the gap between "here's the mockup" and "here's the live site." I close that gap. I can read a design, understand what it's trying to accomplish, and build it in a way that doesn't require a second person to translate.

Most of my client work is in WordPress, though I've done React, PHP, and enough JavaScript to have opinions about it. I've worked with marketing teams, small businesses, and a smattering of education-sector clients. The common thread is that they needed something to work, and then they needed to not have to think about it again.

## The Automation Bit

Over the past few years I've gotten increasingly interested in the places where AI and automation can take repetitive cognitive work off the plate entirely. Not in the "AI will replace developers" sense, which I find boring as a conversation. More in the "I can describe what I want in a markdown file and have an agent do it" sense, which I find genuinely useful.

The homelab posts on this blog describe how that infrastructure works. The short version: a cheap VPS, [Tailscale](https://tailscale.com) for networking, Docker for services, and [Claude Code](https://claude.ai/code) for the agent layer. It's not complicated. It's also not something most people have built, which is why I'm writing about it.

## Why a Blog

Mostly to have somewhere to put the things I've figured out. The web is full of tutorials that assume you're starting from zero. These posts assume you're a working developer who wants to know what I actually did and why, not a walkthrough for someone who's never seen a terminal.
